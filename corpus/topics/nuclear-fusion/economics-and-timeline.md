# Fusion Economics: Can It Actually Be Cheap?

The running joke: fusion is always 30 years away. But $7B+ in private investment since 2021 says the timeline has shifted. The question is no longer just "can we do it?" but "can we do it cheaply enough to matter?"

## The energy landscape fusion enters

Fusion must compete with:

| Source | LCOE (2025, $/MWh) | Characteristics |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Solar (utility) | 25-40 | Intermittent, needs storage |
| Wind (onshore) | 30-50 | Intermittent, needs storage |
| Natural gas (CCGT) | 45-75 | Dispatchable, CO₂ emitting |
| Nuclear fission (new build) | 80-150+ | Dispatchable, baseload, long construction |
| Solar + 4h storage | 50-80 | Partly dispatchable |

Fusion's target LCOE needs to be <$80/MWh to compete on pure cost. But fusion has advantages beyond LCOE:
- **Dispatchable baseload** — unlike solar/wind, runs 24/7 regardless of weather
- **No carbon emissions** — zero operational CO₂
- **No long-lived waste** — after ~100 years, RAFM steel waste decays to clearance levels
- **No meltdown risk** — physically impossible; plasma cools and stops in seconds
- **Essentially unlimited fuel** — deuterium from seawater, lithium is abundant
- **Energy-dense siting** — GW-scale output from a facility on a few acres

These characteristics mean fusion could command a premium over intermittent renewables for grid reliability.

## What drives fusion cost?

### Capital cost (dominant)

Fusion fuel is essentially free. Operating costs are modest. Like fission, it's all about the upfront capital cost and how quickly you build.

**ITER cost reality**: estimated $25-45B+ for a device that won't produce electricity. This is *not* representative of commercial costs — ITER is a one-off physics experiment with 35-nation bureaucracy, designed in the 1990s with 2000s-era magnets.

**Capital cost targets (private companies)**:
- CFS/ARC: targets ~$5-7B for first-of-a-kind, falling to ~$3-4B at scale
- Various startups claim $2-3B targets (unproven)
- For reference: a new AP1000 fission plant costs ~$12-15B in the West

Key cost drivers:
1. **Magnets** (~30% of cost) — HTS REBCO tape price is falling but still expensive. Scale and learning curves matter.
2. **Blanket and first wall** (~15-20%) — must be replaced every 5-7 years (neutron damage). Replacement cost and downtime are recurring expenses.
3. **Balance of plant** (turbine, heat exchangers, cooling, buildings) — mature technology, generally understood
4. **Tritium systems** (~5-10%) — cryogenic distillation, processing, storage
5. **Site, licensing, construction** — potentially the biggest variable

### Capacity factor

A fusion plant that runs 90% of the time (capacity factor 0.9) produces vastly cheaper electricity than one running 50%. Availability is driven by:
- Component lifetime (especially divertor and blanket — replaced every 5-7 years)
- Maintenance downtime (demountable magnets help enormously — ARC's key selling point)
- Reliability of heating systems, cryogenics, tritium processing

ITER-era systems design assumed ~30% availability. Private companies are targeting 70-85%.

### Learning rate

Every energy technology gets cheaper with deployment:
- Solar: ~28% cost reduction per doubling of cumulative capacity
- Wind: ~15%
- Nuclear fission: *negative* learning rate in the West (costs increased with each build) — largely due to bespoke designs, regulatory ratcheting, and loss of supply chain

Fusion optimists argue for a solar-like learning rate because:
- Compact devices enable factory manufacturing (vs. stick-built fission plants)
- Standardized designs avoid the "every plant is unique" fission problem
- HTS magnets are on a steep cost-reduction curve
- Multiple competing approaches drive innovation

Fusion pessimists point out that nuclear technologies face regulatory overhead, public opposition, and supply chain challenges that solar panels don't.

## Private investment timeline

The fusion industry transformed after 2020:

| Period | Private investment | Key events |
|--------|-------------------|------------|
| Pre-2020 | ~$2B total | TAE, General Fusion, Tokamak Energy, small-scale |
| 2021 | ~$2.8B | CFS raises $1.8B (largest single round). Helion raises $500M. |
| 2022-2023 | ~$3B+ | Type One Energy, Zap Energy, Marvel Fusion, multiple Series A/B rounds |
| 2024-2025 | ~$2B+ | Consolidation phase, focus on delivery milestones |
| Total by 2026 | ~$7-8B+ | ~45 companies worldwide |

Government funding is also increasing:
- US DOE: milestone-based public-private partnerships (CFS, Zap, others selected)
- UK: STEP program (government-backed prototype tokamak, ~£2B)
- EU: EUROfusion DEMO conceptual design
- Japan: JT-60SA + public-private framework
- China: CFETR (China Fusion Engineering Test Reactor) — moving fast with domestic supply chain

## The grid integration question

Even if fusion works and is affordable, deploying it requires:

**Regulatory framework**: No country has a licensing regime for fusion power plants. Fusion is not fission — no meltdown, no criticality, minimal waste — but regulators haven't codified this yet.
- NRC (US) is developing a fusion-specific regulatory pathway
- UK has allocated fusion regulation to the Environment Agency (not the nuclear regulator ONR) — a significant signal that fusion should be regulated differently from fission
- Canada, Japan exploring similar approaches

**Grid compatibility**: Fusion plants would likely be 200-500 MWe, dispatchable, good for industrial heat applications (steel, hydrogen, desalination). May be particularly valuable for:
- Replacing retiring coal/gas plants (existing grid connections and cooling infrastructure)
- Industrial process heat (some designs output heat at 600-1000 °C)
- Hydrogen production via high-temperature electrolysis
- Military and remote applications (compact designs)

## Plausible timelines

**Aggressive (private company targets)**:
- 2027-2030: Net energy demonstrations (SPARC, Helion Polaris, others)
- 2030-2035: First pilot plants on grid
- 2035-2040: Commercial deployment begins
- By 2050: Significant contribution to global electricity

**Conservative (mainstream physics community)**:
- 2030s: ITER full operation, net energy demonstrated
- 2040s: DEMO and pilot plants designed and constructed
- 2050s: First commercial fusion electricity
- 2060-2070: Meaningful grid contribution

**The honest answer**: Nobody knows. The technology risk is largely plasma physics + materials. The deployment risk is largely regulation + supply chain + public acceptance. History says big energy transitions take 50+ years, but history also didn't have billions in venture capital and climate urgency.
