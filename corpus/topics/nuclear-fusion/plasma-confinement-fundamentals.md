# Plasma Confinement: The Central Problem of Fusion

The fundamental challenge of fusion energy: hold a plasma at >100 million °C long enough for deuterium and tritium nuclei to fuse, while extracting net energy.

## The Lawson Criterion

John Lawson (1957) showed that net energy gain requires:

$$n \cdot \tau_E \cdot T > \text{threshold}$$

Where:
- **n** = plasma density (particles/m³)
- **τ_E** = energy confinement time (seconds)
- **T** = ion temperature

The "triple product" `n·τ_E·T` must exceed ~3 × 10²¹ keV·s/m³ for D-T fusion. JET achieved Q=0.67 in 1997. ITER targets Q=10. JT-60SA and SPARC are stepping stones.

## Why confinement is hard

Plasma at fusion temperatures is a soup of free ions and electrons. It:
- **Cannot touch walls** — any solid contact instantly cools it and sputters wall material into the plasma (impurity radiation kills performance)
- **Is inherently unstable** — pressure-driven and current-driven MHD instabilities constantly try to tear it apart
- **Has turbulent transport** — microturbulence drives heat and particles outward 10-100× faster than classical collisional transport predicts ("anomalous transport")

## Magnetic confinement approaches

Charged particles gyrate around magnetic field lines. Confine the field lines → confine the plasma.

### Tokamak (most mature)

A toroidal (donut-shaped) chamber with:
- **Toroidal field** — external coils wrap around the torus, creating the primary confining field
- **Poloidal field** — generated by a toroidal plasma current (induced by a central solenoid transformer), creates the twist (rotational transform) needed for stability
- **Vertical field** — additional coils for plasma positioning and shaping

Key parameters:
| Parameter | Symbol | Typical value (ITER) |
|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| Major radius | R₀ | 6.2 m |
| Minor radius | a | 2.0 m |
| Aspect ratio | A = R₀/a | 3.1 |
| Toroidal field | B_T | 5.3 T |
| Plasma current | I_p | 15 MA |
| Pulse length | — | 400 s (inductive) |

**Advantage**: Strong confinement, well-understood physics, highest triple products achieved.  
**Disadvantage**: Pulsed operation (current driven by transformer), disruptions (sudden loss of confinement that can damage the vessel).

### Stellarator (rising contender)

The magnetic twist is produced entirely by external coils — no plasma current needed.

- **Steady-state by design** — no transformer, no disruptions
- **Complex coil geometry** — historically limited by manufacturing precision
- **Wendelstein 7-X** (Greifswald, Germany) is the flagship: optimized coils using computational optimization to minimize neoclassical transport. Achieved record stellarator confinement in 2022-2025.

The W7-X coil shapes were designed by numerically optimizing a cost function over millions of magnetic configurations — one of the earliest major applications of computational optimization in physics engineering.

### Compact approaches

- **Spherical tokamak** (MAST-U, NSTX-U, Tokamak Energy ST-40) — extreme low aspect ratio (A ~ 1.5). Higher β (ratio of plasma pressure to magnetic pressure), but engineering challenges for the center column.
- **Field-reversed configuration (FRC)** — TAE Technologies. Compact, high-β plasmoid. Beam-driven stabilization.
- **Magnetic mirrors** — largely abandoned for mainline fusion but revived by some startups (e.g., Reata Fusion with centrifugal mirrors).

## Key instabilities

| Instability | Type | Danger | Mitigation |
|------------|------|--------|------------|
| Kink | Current-driven MHD | Disruption | Safety factor q > 1 at edge |
| Ballooning | Pressure-driven MHD | Limits achievable β | Plasma shaping (triangularity) |
| Neoclassical tearing modes (NTMs) | Resistive MHD | Performance degradation → disruption | ECCD (electron cyclotron current drive) at the island |
| Edge-localized modes (ELMs) | Edge pressure gradient | Wall erosion, divertor heat loads | RMP coils, pellet pacing, small-ELM regimes |
| Ion temperature gradient (ITG) | Microturbulence | Anomalous transport | Negative triangularity, flow shear |

## H-mode and the pedestal

L-mode (low confinement) → H-mode (high confinement) transition was discovered accidentally on ASDEX in 1982. A transport barrier forms at the plasma edge, creating a steep pressure "pedestal."

H-mode roughly doubles energy confinement time, but the steep edge gradient drives ELMs — periodic explosive bursts that dump energy onto the divertor. Managing ELMs without losing H-mode benefits is one of the biggest open problems.

## Heating methods

To reach ignition temperatures (~15 keV ≈ 174 million °C):

- **Ohmic heating** — plasma current heats resistively, but effectiveness drops as T^{-3/2} (hotter plasma is less resistive). Only good to ~3 keV.
- **Neutral beam injection (NBI)** — accelerate deuterium ions to ~1 MeV, neutralize, inject into plasma. They re-ionize and transfer energy via collisions. ITER will have 33 MW of NBI.
- **Ion cyclotron resonance heating (ICRH)** — RF waves at ion cyclotron frequency (~40-55 MHz for ITER). Minority heating scheme.
- **Electron cyclotron resonance heating (ECRH)** — microwave beams at electron cyclotron frequency (~170 GHz for ITER). Highly localized — also used for current drive and NTM control.

## Diagnostics

You can't stick a thermometer into 100-million-degree plasma. Key diagnostic techniques:

- **Thomson scattering** — laser photons scatter off electrons; Doppler broadening gives T_e, intensity gives n_e
- **Charge exchange recombination spectroscopy (CXRS)** — inject neutral beam, measure light from charge exchange reactions for ion temperature and rotation
- **Interferometry/reflectometry** — electron density profiles from refractive index changes
- **Bolometry** — total radiated power (critical for impurity monitoring)
- **Neutron diagnostics** — DT fusion produces 14.1 MeV neutrons; count rate gives fusion power

## Current status (2026)

- **ITER** — Under construction in Cadarache, France. First plasma originally targeted 2025, now projected ~2034. Will be the first device to produce net fusion energy (Q=10).
- **JT-60SA** — Operational in Naka, Japan since 2023. Largest superconducting tokamak until ITER.
- **SPARC** — Commonwealth Fusion Systems, targeting Q>2 with high-field HTS magnets. First plasma expected ~2026-2027.
- **W7-X** — Continuously setting stellarator performance records. Deuterium campaigns ongoing.
- **MAST-U** — Super-X divertor experiments at Culham, UK.
